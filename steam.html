Union Pasific class 4000 Big Boy

This is the very first model of my carrier other than one or two HO modeling before. And I don’t remember exactly when I started (middle of 70's I believe) or finished. I wanted to make this model as live steam, but because of poor design with no experience in live steaming, it didn’t work. So, I converted it motor driven with battery ( because at that time, both wheels are not insulated each other ). After that, I did a big modification to convert it into electronically controllable locomotive like my other models. Thus, it took me a long time to complete the locomotive, if it is at all completed.

When I got the idea to construct, I found a drawing of locomotive in a magazine ( or to be exact, the drawing made me try) but no drawings about tender. And even having a drawing of the locomotive, I didn’t know which driving bogie rotates relative to body. I found out by looking at pictures ( which was not many in pre Internet days ), but the construction of tender remained unknown to me for a long time. And I made the tender by, perhaps, looking at some of ALCO’s locomotives carefully and thought the diameter of the wheel was 950mm. It later turned out to be 1027mm and I not only replaced the wheels of tender but remade tender chassis as well.

Even then, one thing remained unknown to me - drivers cab has closed environment, that is, there are doors in the back of the cab. That was totally impossible to know by photos. Nobody ever cared about how cab’s doors looked like. It remained an enigma until 1997 when I visited Dallas by business. In Dallas, one Big Boy is preserved at museum. And finally I got the information more than 20 years after I got the idea to make it.

The motor is actually driving the rear engine only. I did try to transmit power to the front engine via universal coupling, but didn't work well and had to abondon.

 

Southern Railway Merchant Navy Class 21C17

The original has three cylinders. So the model above has the third cylinder and the second driving wheel has a center crank. But, of cource, neither of them are visible. Perhaps the axis for the second driving wheel was most difficult to make because it had a crank. However, all the wheels don’t have spokes – which is exceptional for locomotives in Britain. That was one of the reasons I tried this type. I was not sure that spoked driving wheel with that diameter ( about 42mm ) was feasible by my skill.

Above is the angle to show this locomotive’s distinctive style. Smoke deflector is almost a part of body and I wonder if it worked well. Significance of the British locomotives is that no headlights are equipped. I suspect that they drove by watching only signals at night. ( Wasn’t that scary ? ） The original color is malachite green, but that color was not available, and I had to content with some similar color. This colorful paint scheme was also one of the British traditions ( at least in the prime days of steam traction ). Circular plate of “SOUTHERN” gives a very nice accent to otherwise black smoke box door. The plate was made by etched brass which was polished and covered by transparent paint – but now losing it’s color.

The original locomotive was designed by O.V.S.Bulleid who was an ingenious and dauntless designer, incorporated many new ideas into his line of locomotive design. But observing his later designs, he might not be interested in esthetic looks of the locomotives in general. So, this series might have been an exception.

Tender has three wheels supported by sheet springs with single solid chassis and no equalizers are utilized – this is the common British practice. It is a wonder that they have run by very high speed daily with this mechanism. Tracks must have been terribly well maintained.

The cranked driving wheel is shown above, before nickel plating/painting. The driving wheels were made by two pieces. Lathed brass wheel was bored with oval holes and then thin brass circular sheet was soldered to which another holes were bored.

 

Deutsche Bundesbahn Class 01 220

Most attractive features of this locomotive are big driving wheels with thin spokes, almost fragile looking bar frames through which one can see the other side, massive boiler with almost invisible short chimney and Minden style smoke deflectors.

This locomotive was one of the most complicated models I had ever built until the modeling of BR18. All the wheels are insulated with one another so that power and control signals are fed via two rails. Bar frame was modeled as close to the original as possible with dummy sheet springs, but the trailing wheel is mounted on movable frame as opposed to the original. Otherwise, it was impossible to manoeuvre steep curves of r=2000mm.

Another thing notable when making this model was the introduction of CAD for drawings. I thought it was almost impossible to draw the spoked driving wheels by hand, I bought a 486 PC with Win3.1. Win3.1 froze so often that I had to save data in every 30 minutes or less, but even then, the power of CAD was very helpful.

I tried to model bar frame and thin spokes of the driving wheels both of which show the well calculated original design. It is a wonder that even fragile looking frames and wheels are supporting such a massive locomotive. In this model, third driving wheel is motor driven and driving force to first and second wheels is transmitted via connecting rods. I feared that the rotation of the first driving wheel might not be smooth because the plays of the connecting rods would cause phase inaccuracy from the third driving wheel. But it worked successfully after some mechanical adjustment. Boiler and body are made of 0.3mm brass sheets. Frames are made of 1.5mm brass and painted black before assembly.

Maybe the most challenging work was the manufacturing of the wheels. It has twenty spoked discs that really require patience. After having lathed the disc, the lines of spokes are scribed, drilling holes and grinding by hand piece grinder followed. The material must be free-cutting brass, otherwise it is impossible to cut by amateur machinist.

 

NSWR Class AD6039

Among many type of steam locomotives, Garratt locomotive is one of the most distinctive motive power. Continents utilized this type are almost limited to Africa and Australia. Most of the African gauge is 1067mm, there is a problem of scale when modeling for 0 gauge. And information is very much limited. Some pictorial books are available for African steam, but it is almost hopeless to find necessary dimensions let alone the drawings. So, the remained candidates were found in Australia, and Internet was at hand. I got in touch with New South Wales Rail Transport museum, and bought a very nice book via Internet.

AD60 – articulated D60 – may be one of the biggest Garratt locomotives, and I feared a bit of the manoeuvrability of my rails with steep curves, but it worked all right.

Some says Garratt locomotives are ugly – very unusual, that is true. But thinking about the balance of big articulated locomotives, I think Garratt is the best. Two engines can bear almost the same weight – the difficulty of Big Boys were that the front engine gets slippery - center of gravity moves inside when running on curves and ample space for boilers is available which enables to use fat efficient boilers.

I don’t understand why Americans never used this type on their rails – which were not always kept in best conditions. As the fact that African rails were the largest users of this type tells, this type of locomotive can manoevre worse conditioned tracks. And faster express locomotives can be easier constructed compared with Mallet type articulation. French made Algerian Garratt locomotive must have been a sight to see. (And I eventually made this one after a long time.)

Purposes of the components attached to various locations of the locomotive can be sometimes guessed and sometimes not. Although not observable clearly, powered valve gear reversers are equipped to both engines – it is not practical at all to control valve gears mechanically from cabs which moves relative to engines at curves. And there is a cutting off of water tank facing to the smoke box. This is because the smoke box door won’t open without it – I realized while I was making drawings.

 

LNER Class A4 4467 Wild Swan

This is one of the best known steam locomotives not only in Britain but throughout the world (for enthusiasts at least), particularly her sister locomotive “Mallard” holds world speed record of 203.4km/h, although German Class 05 also claims similar speed. However, the beauty of this model may be found in her stream lined body designed by Sir Nigel Gresley. The locomotive was a product of heated competition between the then LNER and LMS. It was designed for express passenger locomotive to serve between London(King’s cross) and Edinburgh'(Waverly) thus equipped by 2032mm driving wheels with three cylinder driving gears, and notably by a “corridor tender” which has a narrow passage to the hauled trains for driving staff change during it’s run,

In modeling, the main focus lies how the body line is represented with formidable three pair of driving wheels. When the original locomotive appeared, the driving wheels and front of the cylinder was covered by a casing so that a straight line is emphasized in level with that of the tender. But it was later removed for better maintenance, revealing her real beauty.

The actual structure of the model is far from the original, not only because the model is driven by electric motors, but also the three cylinder structure is completely omitted ( see BR class 18 for comparison). Yet the model has some contrivance to cope with relatively steep curvature of rail that is common in models. The trailing wheel actually has independent frame from the main one so that the wheel has wider freedom of lateral displacement (same as in the case of DB 01 220).

The corridor tender looked like above. A door at the end of the tender and a small circular window for illumination and knuckle/hook coupler. One of the structures still puzzling me is the four wheel tender with rigid frame and leaf springs rather than bogies with leaf and coil springs. How can it stably operate over 150km/h ? (Not to mention over 200km/h.)

One of the difficulties in modeling is the painting. Unfortunately, I am not talented in painting and I always rely on commercially available spray paint and handiwork when necessary. Thus, the lines separating black and blue livery was drawn manually. The silhouette of the body is the result of soldering pieces of brass cut and bent, and looks more or less all right.

 

du Bousquet 6.024

This locomotive is generally referred to “du Bousquet” locomotive after the inventor Gaston du Bousquet of Nord railways. As seen above, it is a four cylinder compound locomotive, connecting two engines back to back. The structure looks rather unusual, but they were not uncommon particularly in France and were found in China as well. Also, the information of this locomotive is easy to find on the Internet, drawings inclusive. Observe that spring is visible in the buffer housing. This was one of the trickiest points in construction.

“Ceinture” refers to the Grande Ceinture which was an outer circle line of Paris operated on goods and passenger service. The operator Syndicate de Ceintures was dissolved in 1935 and the locomotives were transferred to Nord railways. The mode of operation required a machine that could travel in both directions, having enough tractive effort. This type of locomotive are classified by an “articulated” locomotive like Garratt or Bigboy, but the difference is that the two bogies supporting one body - hence the locomotive cannot be huge in size. In structure, it shares the same idea of modern electric locomotives.

Cab window is coverd by a piece of black paper to conseal control circuit inside. Under the cab shows a bit classic driving gear, particularly two bars supporting the cross head. Since the size of cylinder is smaller than the front ( say, chimny side is front ) bogie, this side is equipped wioth high pressure cylinders.

Coal must have been carried in relatively small space, but the purpose of the locomotive justifies the bunker size, besides it should provide clear rear view for the driver. But it should'nt have been very comfortable for the cabmen.

 

BR 18 451

This locomotive is one of the masterpieces of German design. Originally designed for Bayerische Staatsbahnen as express passenger locomotive and has several variants, the number 451 belongs to a class that has 2000mm driving wheel, and in modeling, this should be preferable. Head lights are illuminated by white LEDs, a pity was that the color was bluish white. Incandescent lamp color was not available at the time of construction.

The looks and style surely justify the machine to be called “one of the most beautiful locomotives”. Big elegant driving wheels as well as see through frames matches with a boiler not too big and stylish chimney. The size of the deflector is just as it should be. Steps hanging in front of the deflector is actually longer for the real locomotive, but in this model it was cut short to provide space to the leading wheels.

The class is a four cylinder compound, meaning that there are two more (smaller) cylinders inside the frame. In case of modeling, it will be very challenging to build four cylinder machine - driving wheel shall have cranks, limited space inside the frame and so on. However, if one tries to produce this model, these points above are imperative, particularly, the frame (called bar frame) has a nice visibility to the inside. ( Reason that I didn’t make class A4 as three cylinder locomotive was that it has plate frame through which nothing can be seen.) And as anticipated, it turned out to be a most complex locomotive I have ever constructed.

Crank and two inner main rods are observable in the picture above. Obviously, the big ends are screw closed.

 

CFA Garratt BT1

Designed for PLM’s Algerian lines ( Algiers - Oran- Constantine) for express passenger service. It started operation in 1936. However, because of the war, their lives were very sadly short. Garratt locomotives are mainly found in Africa, but they are mostly on narrow gauge (1067mm) and inevitably for mixed services, this may be the only standard gauge express Garratt. (AD60 of NSWR is also a standard gauge Garratt.)

Both ends are stream lined and can operate to both direction. The above picture shows water storage first (this is generally thought to be the front end), but the looks are identical in both ends.

One of the most interesting features of this locomotive is the Cossart valve gear. Second driving wheel drives a rod (thick rod with three holes) and eventually rotates a mechanism to control in/out of steam to the cylinders. Although the same valve gear is found on 1-D-1 tank locomotive of Nord railways, the details are difficult to know. However, very interestingly, there is a patent document (US2064293) by L.R.C.Cossart in 1932 on the Internet by which I was able to understand some part of the mechanism. (Who will be interested about the disclosure of this patent document, but me?) Although the Cossart valve gear claims good characteristics, it seems nice amount of power is required to drive the valve mechanism if that thick rod is necessary - was it efficient ?

Complicated link mechanism that is distinctive for this locomotive. It also looks like that the cab is completely closed. This had been operated in Algeria, couldn't it have been hot ?
